Migration & Urbanization

Historically a labour-sending state, Odisha has seen an intensification of out-migration since the 1990s, profoundly reshaping its rural economy. In many southern districts, migration is now a key survival strategy, with remittances forming up to two-thirds of household income, intertwining agriculture with new livelihood patterns.
Odisha's migration story is a complex blend of both distress-driven and aspirational movements, marked by a significant increase in seasonal circular outflows, famously highlighted during the mass reverse migration of COVID-19. As the state possesses a notable demographic dividend and surplus agrarian labour, understanding these dynamics is crucial. This platform aims to analyse current migration and urbanization trends, offering insights for policy to maximize economic benefits, potentially through new urban centres. Crucially, it addresses the urgent need for empirical research on these evolving patterns to inform effective, evidence-based regional development.

How Different Data Sources Measure Migrants

Migration is defined differently by various official sources. Here’s a simple breakdown:

  • Census of India: A person is considered a migrant if they were born in a different place from where they are currently living, and have lived in the current place for at least six months.
  • National Statistical Office (NSO): A person is counted as a migrant if they have moved from another place and stayed for over six months in the current location.

While the Census is the main and most detailed source of migration data, its latest figures are from 2011, making it quite outdated. On the other hand, the PLFS (2020–21) provides more recent data but in less detail. Also, NSO and PLFS often report lower migration numbers than the Census, so their figures should be used carefully when comparing total numbers. Due to the lack of updated Census data, this study mainly uses PLFS 2020–21 to estimate the current number of migrants.
