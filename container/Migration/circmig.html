Short-Term Circular Migration from Odisha:
Young, Rural, and Vulnerable

Shorter duration flow generally takes place on seasonal basis without changing usual place of residence. NSS 64th round and India Human Development Survey (IHDS) collect information on both out-migrating members and short-term circular migrants. The study uses NSS 64th round for estimating the volume and nature of these migrations.
As per census adjusted estimations based on NSS 2007-08, the figure for short-term circular migrants from Odisha was only 4,81,555, comprising 1.2% of the stateâ€™s total population, much lower than that of Bihar (27,07,910), Uttar Pradesh (21,80,736) and West Bengal (16,29,184). Further, NSS-region-wise disaggregated analysis indicated that the Southern region had the highest number of short-term circular out-migrants, i.e., 3,38,108. However, studies have argued for much higher estimations of seasonal flow from the state. CMLS and Ajeevika Bureau conducted a study on 99,523 rural households in coastal and western districts, which revealed that 30.8% of households had at least one seasonal migrant. According to their extrapolation based on the Panchayat Census 2014, these two regions comprise 1.53 million seasonal migrants (CMLS, 2014). Rajshekhar (2015) argued that the number is as high as 2,00,000 only in Balangir district of the state. He also argued that the seasonal outflow has increased in the recent decades.

In contrast with the permanent out-migration, short-term outmigration is mostly rural and dominated by lower socio-economic strata in the state. In 2007-08, 94.3% of short-term migrants originated from rural areas of the state. A large section i.e., 41.3% of short-term migrants from Odisha migrate to rural areas. In 2007-08, only 58.7% migrated to urban areas, much lower compared to 67.9% of the national figure. Rajshekhar (2015) mentioned the mass-scale movement of short-term migrants to brick kilns in rural areas within the state as well as in Andhra Pradesh. In terms of employment composition, it is seen that 34.5% of the migrants were working in manufacturing and 33.6% were engaged in construction sector.
Short-term migrants from Odisha not only comprise the most socio-economically disadvantaged strata of the state, but they are also more disadvantaged compared to their counterparts who originate from other states. NSS estimates reveal that more than half of the short-term migrants belong to the lowest two MPCE quintiles, and the figures were much higher than the national average. Moreover, 38.8% and 23.3% of these short-term migrants belonged to the ST and SC categories, respectively, unlike the dominance of the OBC category (42.2%) at the national level. Moreover, seasonal outmigrants from the state is composed of the young age group. It is seen that more than half of the short-term outmigrants belonged to the age group of 15-29 years. The younger age group and disadvantaged socio-economic conditions make the seasonal out-migrants from the state very vulnerable at their destinations.
