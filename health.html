Health

Odisha's demographic transition reveals regional disparities, with population growth driven by increased longevity, resulting in an older median age. The shrinking working-age population and rising age dependency ratio are expected to reduce the state's demographic dividend by 2026, creating financial and administrative challenges. Approximately 80% of Odisha's population resides in rural areas and relies on primary healthcare services. With a significant 23% Scheduled Tribe and 17% Scheduled Caste populations, Odisha is rich in demographic, cultural, linguistic, and socio-economic diversity. Despite recent improvements, the diversity coupled with existing health sector challenges complicates efforts to provide tailored healthcare across varied geographies and needs.
This chapter examines the health and nutrition concerns arising from the evolving demographic transition, as well as the pre-existing challenges in Odisha. It also discusses the preparedness required for a healthy and productive Odisha by 2036.

Hypertension and Diabetes on the Rise: Odisha’s Cross-Generational Health Crisis

Odisha faces a growing health challenge as non-communicable diseases like hypertension and diabetes rise alongside an aging population. By 2036, over 10.8 million people may have hypertension, and over 4.1 million may have diabetes. These chronic conditions affect both elderly and younger adults, straining healthcare resources.

Below Replacement, But Not Beyond Risk: The Silent Crisis of Child Mortality in Odisha

Though Odisha has achieved replacement-level fertility, child mortality and nutrition gaps remain, especially in rural and tribal areas. These reflect persistent issues in health and service delivery. Improving child health systems is vital to harness the demographic dividend.

One-Third of Odisha’s Children Battling Malnutrition: A Dual Burden of Deficiency and Excess

While undernutrition among children under five has declined in Odisha, anaemia and childhood overweight are rising, creating a double burden of malnutrition. District-level disparities persist, threatening health equity and long-term human development.

Changing Populations, Unchanged Rights? SRHR in Odisha Today

Contraceptive use in Odisha has risen (practice: 42%→62%, usage: 57%→74%), but inequalities by caste, education, and wealth persist. Younger, educated women show limited agency, reflecting hidden social barriers. With a shrinking youth population post-2021, youth SRHR may be sidelined, risking future health outcomes.

Inequality in the Womb: Maternal Health Disparities in Odisha

Odisha faces serious maternal health challenges, with a high Maternal Mortality Ratio, especially among tribal and rural women. Low antenatal care coverage and early pregnancies worsen risks, particularly in districts like Mayurbhanj. Nearly 24% of girls with no schooling begin childbearing early, compared to just 4.6% among the well-educated.

Addiction and Abuse: Undermining Odisha’s Human Capital

Tobacco and alcohol use remain alarmingly high in Odisha, with growing health and social costs. Over 50% of men and 26% of women use tobacco, far above national levels, contributing to a rising toll of oral cancer. Simultaneously, substance abuse fuels domestic violence, affecting nearly 1 in 3 women. These issues hinder women’s participation in the workforce, weaken family well-being, and erode the state’s potential to harness its demographic dividend.

Odisha’s Double Burden of Malnutrition: Nutritional Challenges Across the Life Course

Odisha is facing a dual nutritional crisis marked by persistent undernutrition and rising obesity, reflecting the broader double burden of malnutrition. While underweight prevalence is declining, anaemia, especially among women, remains alarmingly high. Concurrently, overweight and obesity are rising among adults, mirroring demographic shifts. Among the elderly, Odisha records one of the highest rates of undernutrition in India, signalling a lifelong continuity of poor nutrition.
